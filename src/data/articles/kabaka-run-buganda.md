The Buganda Kingdom’s annual Kabaka Birthday Run took place on February 23, 2025, drawing over 50,000 participants to honor Kabaka Ronald Muwenda Mutebi II, as celebrated on March 1. The 10-kilometer event in Kampala blended sport with tradition, with runners donning kitenge and barkcloth attire. Proceeds support health initiatives, this year targeting sickle cell disease awareness. Social media lit up with photos of the colorful throng, showcasing Buganda’s enduring cultural pull.

The run, a fixture since 2014, reinforces the kingdom’s role in modern Uganda, where monarchies hold symbolic sway. Dancers, drummers, and royal guards lined the route, reviving traditions like the _amaggunju_ dance. King Mutebi, though absent due to health, sent a message thanking participants, a moment shared widely on X. For many, it’s a rare chance to connect with heritage amid rapid urbanization.

Beyond festivity, the event bridges divides. Politicians, youth, and diaspora Ugandans joined, signaling unity in a politically charged climate. Organizers hailed its growth, with international runners from Kenya and Ethiopia adding flair. Critics, however, note the kingdom’s limited political power under Museveni’s rule, calling it a cultural relic.

As of March 1, 2025, the Kabaka Run stands as Buganda’s loudest cultural heartbeat. It’s a day of pride, sweat, and solidarity—proof that tradition can still rally a nation.
