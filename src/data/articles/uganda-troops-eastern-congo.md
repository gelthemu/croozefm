Ugandan forces escalated their presence in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) on February 18, 2025, deploying troops to Bunia to quell ethnic violence, as reported on March 1. The Uganda People’s Defence Force (UPDF) aims to stabilize the mineral-rich region near Lake Albert, targeting groups like the Allied Democratic Forces (ADF) with Islamic State ties. UPDF spokesman Felix Kulayigye called it a “necessary intervention,” but the move stirs unease about a broader conflict. X posts reflect mixed views—support for security meets fears of overreach.

Since 2021, Uganda has operated in DRC under a bilateral pact, hunting rebels threatening both nations. The latest deployment follows clashes between Lendu farmers and Hema pastoralists, echoing the 1990s war when foreign armies, including Uganda’s, fueled chaos. Analysts warn of history repeating if tensions with DRC’s M23 rebels or Rwanda escalate, though Uganda insists its focus is peacekeeping.

For Ugandans, the operation highlights military muscle but also risks. Soldiers face harsh terrain and hostile groups, while back home, some question the cost amid domestic needs. Congo’s government welcomes the support, yet local distrust of foreign troops persists, complicating the mission.

As of March 1, 2025, Bunia holds steady, but the deployment’s success hinges on diplomacy as much as firepower. Uganda’s role in DRC underscores its regional ambitions—and the fragile balance of peace in a volatile zone.
