On February 26, 2025, Ugandan authorities announced the repatriation of one of Joseph Kony’s wives and three of his children from the Central African Republic, a development stirring debate as of March 1. Kony, the elusive leader of the Lord’s Resistance Army (LRA), remains at large, wanted by the International Criminal Court for war crimes committed during his decades-long insurgency. The family’s return, facilitated by regional cooperation, offers a rare glimpse into the aftermath of his reign of terror, which displaced millions in northern Uganda.

Officials say the repatriation aims to reintegrate Kony’s relatives into society, though details about their identities and conditions remain scarce. Some Ugandans see this as a step toward closure, while others question why Kony himself evades capture despite weakened LRA activity. On X, opinions split—some demand justice, others wonder if his family’s return signals a deal in the works. The government insists the hunt continues, with military efforts ongoing in neighboring countries.

The move has political undertones. President Museveni, who has long touted victories over the LRA, may use this to bolster his image ahead of the 2026 election. Critics, however, argue it highlights unresolved trauma in the north, where communities still grapple with Kony’s legacy. Humanitarian groups call for support for returnees, noting the psychological scars borne by those tied to the LRA.

As of March 1, 2025, Kony’s whereabouts remain unknown, fueling speculation about his survival or demise. His family’s return could pressure remaining LRA factions or renew focus on reconciliation. For now, it’s a poignant reminder of a dark chapter Uganda seeks to move beyond—but cannot fully escape.
