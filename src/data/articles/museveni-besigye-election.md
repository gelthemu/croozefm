President Yoweri Museveni has rejected growing calls to release opposition leader Kizza Besigye, insisting on a “quick trial” to reveal alleged crimes as of March 1, 2025. Besigye’s detention since November 2024, now marked by a hunger strike and treason charges, has fueled protests and international criticism. Museveni, in power since 1986, framed the case as a matter of national security in a recent statement, dismissing forgiveness as an option despite Besigye’s frail health. This stance comes as Uganda gears up for a 2026 election where Museveni is expected to run again.

The government’s hardline position contrasts with a recent Supreme Court ruling deeming military trials of civilians unconstitutional—a decision Museveni called “wrong” but reluctantly followed by shifting Besigye’s case to a civilian court. Critics see this as a tactic to silence dissent, pointing to a pattern of opposition crackdowns. On X, users debate Museveni’s legacy, with some praising his stability and others decrying authoritarianism, especially as Bobi Wine, another opposition figure, warns of Besigye’s nearing death.

Regional and global voices amplify the pressure. The Commonwealth’s February 18 plea for Besigye’s release highlighted democratic backsliding, while East African activists plan further demonstrations. Analysts warn that Museveni’s refusal to compromise could inflame tensions, potentially destabilizing Uganda at a time when his long rule faces fresh scrutiny from younger voters and a restless opposition.

For now, Museveni’s grip appears firm, backed by loyal security forces and a government narrative of thwarting threats. Yet, Besigye’s plight—echoed in chants across Kampala—may yet shift public sentiment. As the election nears, this showdown could define whether Uganda tilts toward reconciliation or deeper division.
